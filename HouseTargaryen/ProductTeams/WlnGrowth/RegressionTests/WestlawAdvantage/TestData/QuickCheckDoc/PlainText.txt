Guido’s Employment Agreement with BMI states: “Any dispute, claim or controversy arising out of or relating to this Agreement or the breach, termination, enforcement, interpretation or validity thereof, including the determination of the scope or applicability of this agreement to arbitrate, shall be determined by arbitration in Chicago, Illinois before one arbitrator.”  Emp’t Agmt. at 10, § 6.07 (attached as Exhibit C).  Courts have found similarly worded arbitration clauses contained in employment agreements to encompass defamation claims asserted against former employers.  See Hangebrauck v. Ernst & Young, LLP, 2017 IL App (1st) 153430-U, §§ 36-41 (compelling arbitration of plaintiff’s defamation claim where “any question as to whether plaintiff’s claims fall within the scope of the arbitration provision is to be determined by an arbitrator”); see also Perik v. JP Morgan Chase Bank, U.S.A., 2011 IL App (1st) 093088-U, §§ 13, 36 (compelling arbitration of plaintiff’s libel per se claim where contract’s arbitration provision required binding arbitration of “any claim . . . arising from or relating in any way to this Agreement”).  Guido’s defamation per se claim likewise “relat[es] to” the Employment Agreement and is therefore subject to mandatory arbitration.  The Court should therefore dismiss BMI and order Guido and BMI to arbitrate the defamation claim. Even if the Court finds that Veleris sharing the July 8 Letter with Garifalis constitutes “publication,” Guido’s defamation per se claim still fails because it is barred by Veleris’ qualified privilege. To determine whether a qualified privilege exists, courts look to “whether the occasion created some recognized duty or interest to make the communication so as to make it privileged.”  Coghlan v. Beck, 2013 IL App (1st) 120891, ¶ 43 (quoting Kuwik v. Starmark Star Mktg. & Admin., Inc., 156 Ill. 2d 16, 27 (1993)).  “The elements of a qualified privilege are: ‘(1) good faith by the writer; (2) interest or duty to be upheld; (3) a statement limited in its scope to that purpose; (4) a proper occasion; and (5) publication in a proper manner and to the proper parties only.’”  Rupcich v. United Food & Commercial Workers Int’l Union Local 881, 69 F. Supp. 3d 889, 904-05 (N.D. Ill. 2014) (quoting Phillips v. Univ. of Chi. Hosps. & Clinics, 137 Ill. App. 3d 485, 489 (1st Dist. 1985)), rev’d on other grounds, 833 F.3d 847 (7th Cir. 2016).  “A court must weigh the value of the type of interest to be protected against the degree of damage to be expected from release of the type of defamatory matter involved.”  Id. at 905 (citation and internal quotation marks omitted).  A qualified privilege provides absolute immunity from suit and is a question of law, meaning it is ripe for adjudication at the motion to dismiss stage.  See Pompa v. Swanson, 2013 IL App (2d) 120911, ¶ 27. Here, Veleris’ allegedly defamatory statements to Garifalis are indisputably protected by a qualified privilege.  Veleris suspected that Guido was engaging in misconduct in the course of his employment with Belwith Product and BMI.  He, along with Garifalis, investigated Guido’s misconduct and ultimately determined that his employment with Belwith Products and BMI had to be terminated.  (See Exhibits A and B.)  Thereafter, Veleris and Garifalis jointly drafted the July 8 Letter to Guido explaining the bases of his termination for cause.  (Id.)  Veleris wrote the July 8 Letter in good faith, the letter was limited in scope to the bases for Guido’s termination, and was only shared with Garifalis in his capacity as a member of senior leadership of BMI, and because Garifalis had played an active role in the investigation of the circumstances that led to Guido’s termination for cause.  (Id.)  See Coghlan, 2013 IL App (1st) 120891, ¶ 55 (finding qualified privilege where defendant was investigating plaintiff, a fellow board member accused of wrongdoing).  Furthermore, the July 8 Letter specifically addressed Guido’s inappropriate workplace behavior, including bullying other employees.  (See Am. Countercl. Exhibit A at 2.)  These types of company communications – addressing employee bullying, harassment, and other inappropriate workplace behavior – fit squarely into the privilege.  See Gerrard v. Garda Sec., Inc., 2011 WL 3511481, at *1-4, 9 (C.D. Ill. Aug. 11, 2011) (finding qualified privilege for communications surrounding investigation and discipline of supervisor with an “abusive, aggressive, harassing, [and] bullying . . . style”). For these reasons, the Court should dismiss Guido’s Amended Counterclaim
